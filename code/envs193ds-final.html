##1b. Figure needed They can use a boxplot to visualize these results.   1. Precipitation was not a strong predictor of flooded wetland area in the San Joaquin River Delta (simple linear regression, F(df model, df error) = test statistic, \(R^2\) = effect size, p = 0.11, \(\alpha\) = significance level).

d. Interpretation

Another variable that could influence the flooded area of a wetland is local wetland management practices. This variable would be categorical (managed vs unmanaged) or ordinal (levels of management/intervention). For example, higher water retention due to lack of vegetation in wetlands could increase flooding.

Problem 3. Data analysis

3a. Response variable

The response variable is Mountain Plover occupancy. In this data set, the 1s and 0s are binary numbers that represent mountain plover nest presence/absence at a given nesting site when surveyed. A 1 means the species nest was observed while a 0 means there was no nests present at the time of the survey.

3b. Purpose of study

Visual obstruction

Visual obstruction is a continuous variable where each observation is an individual measurement, explained in the data collection subsection of the methods section in paragraph 3. As visual obstruction increases, the probability of Mountain Plover nest site use decreases because Mountain Plovers tend to prefer flat landscapes with bare ground exposure and sparse vegetation (found in paragraph 2 of the introduction).

Distance to prairie dog colony edge

Distance to prairie dog colony edge is also a continuous variable, found in the modeling framework subsection of the methods section in paragraph 1. As distance to colony edge increases, the probability of Mountain Plover nest site decreases because vegetation height and shrub cover are higher outside of prairie dog colonies where Mountain Plovers prefer to nest (found in the 3rd paragraph of the discussion).

3j. Interpret your results

The predicted probability of Mountain Plover nest site occupancy at no visual obstruction (0) is 0.73, indicating that Mountain Plovers tend to use nests where there is less visual obstruction, supporting the negative relationship seen in Figure 1. This is consistent with the observations found in 3b; Mountain Plovers prefer to nest in open and sparsely vegetated habitats with less obstructions because it allows for higher visibility of predators. This is also in accordance with distance to prairie dog colony edge; the farther from the colony, the less visual obstructions and sparser the vegetation is, which means the probability of Mountain Plover nesting is greater.

In homework 2, I visualized my data using bar graphs and a scatterplot. In homework 3, I did the same thing, but updated the data with all of my observations. In the bar graph, the final graph showed the full range of observations; with the limited data in homework 2, it showed that Monday had the highest number of steps, but in my final visualization we see that I had the greatest number of steps on Thursdays. There was also a different pattern on my scatterplot. The initial plot showed a decrease in step count over time, but the final plot shows that there is no visible correlation between date and steps taken (non-linear). I think that this data would be better visualized by a boxplot instead, so if we were to make another visualization, that’s what I would do (which is also feedback I got from my classmates during week 9 workshop). In my affective visualization, you can see the pattern/correlation between day of the week and step count better, like in the bar graph, though the bar graph uses total step count for the day and the affective visualization uses individual/daily counts. The scatterplot is the only visualization by which you can’t see the pattern, and that is because my predictor variable was the date, which would not make sense in predicting step count. For a future visualization, I would like to keep day of the week as the predictor, but break it up into 2 categories – weekday and weekend –, which I think would give us a better analysis.

I think that an appropriate analysis for this would be a two-sample t-test to test whether my daily step count differs between weekdays vs weekends. This analysis would be suitable because step count is continuous and the predictor (day type) is categorical.

Daily step count differed significantly between weekdays and weekend, with more activity (steps) on weekdays versus weekends.
